A Gentle Introduction to Lyapunov Stability

Mathematics May 28, 2026 15 min read

Introduction #

If you've studied any dynamical systems or control theory, you've encountered the word "stability" approximately ten thousand times. It's one of those concepts that feels completely intuitive — of course a ball in a bowl is stable and a ball on a hilltop is not — until the moment you try to write down a precise definition. Then things get subtle.

The theory we're about to explore, developed by the Russian mathematician Aleksandr Lyapunov in his 1892 doctoral thesis, provides a remarkably powerful framework for analyzing stability without solving the differential equation. Read that again. We can determine whether solutions converge, diverge, or stay bounded — without ever finding a closed-form solution. In a field where most nonlinear ODEs are unsolvable analytically, this is nothing short of a superpower.

In this post, we'll build up Lyapunov stability theory from the ground level. We'll start with physical intuition, formalize it into precise definitions, and then develop the machinery to actually use it. By the end, you'll have a working understanding of Lyapunov functions, the direct method, LaSalle's invariance principle, and how to apply all of it to a concrete example.

What Do We Mean by Stability? #

Before we dive into formalism, let's build some physical intuition. Consider three scenarios:

A ball in a bowl. Push the ball slightly off the bottom and it rolls back. Push it harder and it oscillates, but eventually (with friction) returns to rest at the bottom. This is stable — small perturbations don't cause the ball to leave the neighborhood of the equilibrium.

A ball on a hilltop. The slightest nudge and the ball rolls away, never to return. This is unstable — no matter how small the perturbation, the ball eventually ends up far from where it started.

A ball on a flat surface. Push the ball and it stays wherever you pushed it. It doesn't return to where it was, but it doesn't run away either. This is sometimes called neutrally stable or marginally stable.

These three mental images will guide us as we translate physical intuition into mathematics. Notice that stability is fundamentally about what happens after a perturbation — it's a property of how the system responds to being disturbed from equilibrium.

Equilibrium Points #

Consider an autonomous ordinary differential equation:

$$\dot{x} = f(x)$$

where $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is the state vector and $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ is a continuously differentiable vector field. An equilibrium point $x^*$ is a state where the system is at rest:

$$f(x^*) = 0$$

If the system starts at $x^*$, it stays there forever — there's no velocity to carry it away. Equilibrium points are the fixed points of the dynamical system.

For example, consider the simple pendulum (without friction):

$$\dot{\theta} = \omega, \quad \dot{\omega} = -\frac{g}{L}\sin\theta$$

Setting both derivatives to zero gives $\omega = 0$ and $\sin\theta = 0$, yielding two equilibria: $(\theta, \omega) = (0, 0)$ (hanging down) and $(\theta, \omega) = (\pi, 0)$ (inverted). Our intuition correctly tells us that the first is stable and the second is unstable. Lyapunov theory will give us the tools to prove this rigorously.

Without loss of generality, we'll often assume $x^* = 0$. This is just a coordinate shift — if the equilibrium is at some other point $x^*$, define $\tilde{x} = x - x^*$ and work with the shifted system.

Lyapunov Stability (Formal Definition) #

Now let's make our intuition precise. The formal definition uses the $\epsilon$-$\delta$ language that will feel familiar if you've studied real analysis.

Definition (Lyapunov Stability). The equilibrium point $x^* = 0$ of $\dot{x} = f(x)$ is stable in the sense of Lyapunov if, for every $\epsilon > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that

$$\|x(0)\| < \delta \implies \|x(t)\| < \epsilon \quad \text{for all } t \geq 0.$$

In plain language: no matter how tight a ball you draw around the origin (the $\epsilon$-ball), I can find a smaller ball (the $\delta$-ball) such that any trajectory starting inside the $\delta$-ball stays inside the $\epsilon$-ball forever.

This is exactly our "ball in a bowl" intuition, formalized. Note what this does not say — it doesn't require the trajectory to converge to the origin. It only requires that trajectories starting nearby stay nearby. A frictionless pendulum oscillating around the bottom satisfies this definition: the oscillations stay bounded, but the pendulum doesn't come to rest.

The equilibrium is unstable if it is not stable — that is, if there exists some $\epsilon > 0$ for which no $\delta$ works. Some trajectories, no matter how close they start, eventually escape the $\epsilon$-ball.

Asymptotic Stability #

Stability alone is often not enough. In engineering, we typically want the system to not just stay near the equilibrium, but actually converge to it. This motivates a stronger notion.

Definition (Asymptotic Stability). The equilibrium $x^* = 0$ is asymptotically stable if:

  1. It is stable (in the Lyapunov sense), and
  2. There exists $\delta > 0$ such that $\|x(0)\| < \delta$ implies $\lim_{t \to \infty} x(t) = 0$.

Asymptotic stability is our "ball in a bowl with friction." The ball doesn't just stay in the bowl — it actually comes to rest at the bottom. This is the property we almost always want when designing controllers.

Note that condition 2 alone (convergence) is not sufficient. We also need condition 1 (boundedness). There exist pathological systems where trajectories converge to the origin as $t \to \infty$ but first make large excursions away from it. Requiring stability prevents this.

If the convergence in condition 2 holds for all initial conditions $x(0) \in \mathbb{R}^n$, we say the equilibrium is globally asymptotically stable. This is the gold standard — the system converges to the equilibrium from anywhere in state space.

Lyapunov's Direct Method #

Here comes the main result — and in my opinion, one of the most beautiful theorems in applied mathematics.

The key insight is this: if you can find a scalar function that behaves like "energy" — positive everywhere except at the equilibrium, and non-increasing along trajectories — then the equilibrium must be stable. You don't need to solve the ODE. You just need to find the right function.

Definition (Lyapunov Function). A continuously differentiable function $V: D \to \mathbb{R}$ (where $D$ is a neighborhood of the origin) is a Lyapunov function for $\dot{x} = f(x)$ if:

  1. $V(0) = 0$,
  2. $V(x) > 0$ for all $x \in D \setminus \{0\}$ (positive definite),
  3. $\dot{V}(x) \leq 0$ for all $x \in D$ (negative semi-definite along trajectories).

Here $\dot{V}$ is the time derivative along solutions of the ODE, computed via the chain rule:

$$\dot{V}(x) = \nabla V(x) \cdot f(x) = \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\partial V}{\partial x_i} f_i(x)$$

Notice that we compute $\dot{V}$ without knowing $x(t)$ — we just need $f(x)$, the vector field itself. This is the power of the direct method.

Theorem (Lyapunov's Stability Theorem). If there exists a Lyapunov function $V$ on a neighborhood $D$ of the origin for $\dot{x} = f(x)$, then the origin is stable.

Theorem (Lyapunov's Asymptotic Stability Theorem). If, additionally, $\dot{V}(x) < 0$ for all $x \in D \setminus \{0\}$ (strictly negative definite), then the origin is asymptotically stable.

Proof Sketch (Stability)

The proof idea is elegant. Given $\epsilon > 0$, consider the set $\Omega_c = \{x : V(x) \leq c\}$ for some value $c > 0$ chosen so that $\Omega_c$ fits inside the $\epsilon$-ball. Since $V$ is positive definite and continuous, and $V(0) = 0$, such a $c$ exists.

Now, because $\dot{V} \leq 0$, the function $V$ is non-increasing along trajectories. So if a trajectory starts inside $\Omega_c$ (meaning $V(x(0)) \leq c$), then $V(x(t)) \leq V(x(0)) \leq c$ for all $t \geq 0$. The trajectory can never escape $\Omega_c$.

Finally, choose $\delta > 0$ so that $\|x\| < \delta$ implies $V(x) < c$ (possible by continuity of $V$). Then any trajectory starting in the $\delta$-ball stays in $\Omega_c$, which is contained in the $\epsilon$-ball. This is exactly the definition of stability. $\square$

The Lyapunov function acts as a "certificate" of stability. It provides a constructive proof that trajectories can't escape, by giving you a set of nested sublevel sets $\Omega_c$ that are positively invariant.

Finding Lyapunov Functions #

The theory is beautiful, but it raises a practical question: how do you actually find a Lyapunov function? This is where art meets science.

There is no general algorithm that works for all systems (and in fact, the problem is computationally hard in general). But several important special cases have clean solutions.

Linear Systems

For linear systems $\dot{x} = Ax$ where $A$ is Hurwitz (all eigenvalues have negative real part), there is a systematic approach. Consider the quadratic Lyapunov function:

$$V(x) = x^T P x$$

where $P$ is a symmetric positive definite matrix. Its time derivative is:

$$\dot{V}(x) = x^T(A^T P + PA)x = -x^T Q x$$

For $\dot{V}$ to be negative definite, we need $Q = -(A^T P + PA)$ to be positive definite. This leads to the Lyapunov equation:

$$A^T P + PA = -Q$$

Given any $Q \succ 0$, if $A$ is Hurwitz, there exists a unique $P \succ 0$ solving this equation. The converse also holds: if we find $P \succ 0$ for some $Q \succ 0$, then $A$ must be Hurwitz. This is a complete characterization of stability for linear systems.

Mechanical Systems

For mechanical systems, the natural candidate is the total energy:

$$V = T + V_{\text{potential}}$$

or sometimes a modified energy function. If the system has damping (energy dissipation), the total energy is non-increasing along trajectories, making it a natural Lyapunov function. This is the deep connection between Lyapunov theory and physics — the Lyapunov function is the generalized energy.

Variable Gradient Method

For more general nonlinear systems, the variable gradient method assumes $\nabla V = \alpha(x)$ for some function $\alpha$ and imposes conditions that ensure $V$ is positive definite and $\dot{V}$ is negative (semi-)definite. This is a systematic but often laborious approach.

LaSalle's Invariance Principle #

There's a common frustration when applying Lyapunov's theorem: you find a function $V$ that is positive definite and has $\dot{V} \leq 0$ — but $\dot{V}$ is only negative semi-definite, not strictly negative definite. The basic theorem only gives you stability, not asymptotic stability.

LaSalle's Invariance Principle, proved by Joseph LaSalle in 1960, saves the day. It tells us that we can often conclude asymptotic stability even when $\dot{V}$ is only negative semi-definite, as long as the set where $\dot{V} = 0$ doesn't contain any full trajectories (other than the equilibrium).

Theorem (LaSalle's Invariance Principle). Let $\Omega \subset D$ be a compact positively invariant set. Let $V: D \to \mathbb{R}$ be continuously differentiable with $\dot{V}(x) \leq 0$ for all $x \in \Omega$. Define:

$$S = \{x \in \Omega : \dot{V}(x) = 0\}$$

Let $M$ be the largest invariant set contained in $S$. Then every trajectory starting in $\Omega$ converges to $M$ as $t \to \infty$.

The intuition: trajectories can only "linger" where $\dot{V} = 0$. If the only invariant subset of $\{\dot{V} = 0\}$ is the origin, then every trajectory must converge to the origin.

This is enormously useful in practice. For a damped pendulum, the energy derivative $\dot{V}$ is zero whenever the velocity is zero (no damping force). But the only trajectory that keeps the velocity identically zero is the equilibrium itself. So LaSalle tells us the pendulum is asymptotically stable — even though $\dot{V}$ isn't strictly negative definite everywhere.

A Worked Example: The Damped Pendulum #

Let's put everything together with a concrete example. Consider the simple pendulum with viscous damping:

$$\dot{\theta} = \omega$$ $$\dot{\omega} = -\frac{g}{L}\sin\theta - b\omega$$

where $b > 0$ is the damping coefficient. The equilibrium at $(\theta, \omega) = (0, 0)$ (pendulum hanging down) is our candidate for asymptotic stability.

Step 1: Choose a Lyapunov Function

The natural choice is the total energy (shifted so that $V(0) = 0$):

$$V(\theta, \omega) = \frac{1}{2}\omega^2 + \frac{g}{L}(1 - \cos\theta)$$

The first term is kinetic energy and the second is potential energy (with the minimum at $\theta = 0$).

Step 2: Verify Positive Definiteness

Clearly $V(0, 0) = 0$. For $({\theta}, \omega) \neq (0, 0)$ in a neighborhood of the origin (say $|\theta| < \pi$), we have $\frac{1}{2}\omega^2 \geq 0$ and $\frac{g}{L}(1 - \cos\theta) \geq 0$, with equality in both only at the origin. So $V > 0$ on $D \setminus \{0\}$. ✓

Step 3: Compute $\dot{V}$

$$\dot{V} = \frac{\partial V}{\partial \theta}\dot{\theta} + \frac{\partial V}{\partial \omega}\dot{\omega} = \frac{g}{L}\sin\theta \cdot \omega + \omega\left(-\frac{g}{L}\sin\theta - b\omega\right)$$

Expanding and simplifying:

$$\dot{V} = \frac{g}{L}\omega\sin\theta - \frac{g}{L}\omega\sin\theta - b\omega^2 = -b\omega^2$$

Beautiful! The gravity terms cancel perfectly (as they must — conservative forces don't dissipate energy). We're left with $\dot{V} = -b\omega^2 \leq 0$.

Step 4: Apply LaSalle's Principle

Since $\dot{V} = -b\omega^2$ is only negative semi-definite (it's zero whenever $\omega = 0$, regardless of $\theta$), we can't directly conclude asymptotic stability from the basic theorem. But LaSalle rescues us.

The set $S = \{(\theta, \omega) : \dot{V} = 0\} = \{(\theta, \omega) : \omega = 0\}$. What invariant trajectories live entirely in $S$? If $\omega(t) = 0$ for all $t$, then $\dot{\omega}(t) = 0$, which from the ODE gives $\sin\theta(t) = 0$, so $\theta(t) = 0$ (in our domain $|\theta| < \pi$). The only invariant set in $S$ is the origin.

By LaSalle's Invariance Principle, the origin is asymptotically stable. ✓

Visualizing the Phase Portrait

Let's simulate and visualize the phase portrait to build more intuition:

Python 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy.integrate import solve_ivp

def damped_pendulum(t, state, g=9.81, L=1.0, b=0.3):
    """Damped pendulum dynamics."""
    theta, omega = state
    dtheta = omega
    domega = -(g / L) * np.sin(theta) - b * omega
    return [dtheta, domega]

# Simulate from several initial conditions
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(8, 6))
t_span = (0, 20)
t_eval = np.linspace(*t_span, 1000)

# Grid of initial conditions
for theta0 in np.linspace(-2.5, 2.5, 8):
    for omega0 in np.linspace(-4, 4, 6):
        sol = solve_ivp(
            damped_pendulum, t_span, [theta0, omega0],
            t_eval=t_eval, rtol=1e-8
        )
        ax.plot(sol.y[0], sol.y[1], 'steelblue', 
                alpha=0.4, linewidth=0.7)

# Mark the stable equilibrium
ax.plot(0, 0, 'ro', markersize=8, zorder=5, 
        label='Stable equilibrium')

ax.set_xlabel(r'$\theta$ (rad)', fontsize=12)
ax.set_ylabel(r'$\omega$ (rad/s)', fontsize=12)
ax.set_title('Phase Portrait: Damped Pendulum', fontsize=14)
ax.legend(fontsize=10)
ax.set_xlim(-3, 3)
ax.set_ylim(-5, 5)
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

The phase portrait reveals the spiral structure: trajectories spiral inward toward the origin, confirming asymptotic stability. The spiraling (rather than direct approach) reflects the oscillatory nature of the underdamped pendulum. Each orbit crosses the $\omega = 0$ line (where $\dot{V} = 0$) twice per cycle, but it doesn't stay there — it passes through, losing energy each time.

Conclusion #

Lyapunov stability theory gives us a way to reason about the long-term behavior of dynamical systems without solving them. The core ideas are:

  • Stability means trajectories that start nearby stay nearby.
  • Asymptotic stability adds convergence to the equilibrium.
  • Lyapunov functions are generalized energy functions that certify stability through their monotonic decrease along trajectories.
  • LaSalle's Invariance Principle extends the method to cases where the Lyapunov function's derivative is only negative semi-definite.

What makes this theory so enduring is its generality. It applies to nonlinear systems, infinite-dimensional systems (with some extensions), stochastic systems, and discrete-time systems. The same conceptual framework — find a function that decreases along trajectories — underlies an enormous range of results in modern control theory, optimization, and dynamical systems.

In control design, Lyapunov theory is the foundation of Control Lyapunov Functions (CLFs), which let us systematically design stabilizing controllers. The idea is to choose the control input so that a candidate Lyapunov function becomes decreasing — essentially synthesizing a controller and its stability proof simultaneously.

Lyapunov's insight was that stability is not about trajectories — it's about energy. Find the right notion of energy, show it dissipates, and the rest follows.

In my previous post on the Furuta pendulum, we used LQR without discussing why it stabilizes the system. The answer, of course, is Lyapunov theory: the LQR cost function itself serves as a Lyapunov function for the closed-loop system. The connections run deep.
